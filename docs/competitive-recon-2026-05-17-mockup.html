Bottom Line

Buy it if you want convenience, skip it if you want gallery-quality photos.

The Flex-M is the smart starting point if you want cellular trail-cam photos without committing to a monthly bill. The dual-SIM auto-carrier-detection solves the most common cellular failure mode, and the free 100-photo plan covers casual backyard use indefinitely. The caveat is image quality under motion, which is just-okay rather than great.

Who This Is For

Homeowners and casual wildlife watchers who want the cellular convenience of photos delivered to a phone, are willing to accept just-okay image quality in exchange, and prefer a camera that runs on a free or low-cost data plan rather than a recurring monthly subscription.

Buy it if

  • You want photos delivered to your phone without walking to the camera
  • Your property has decent cell coverage on either AT&T or Verizon
  • A free 100-photo monthly plan covers your expected backyard volume
  • You'd rather pay once for the camera than commit to a monthly subscription

Don't buy it if

  • You want gallery-quality stills you'd be proud to print
  • Your property has no cellular coverage on either carrier
  • You want a non-cellular SD-card cam with the best image quality possible
  • You don't want any recurring data plan, even a free tier

What It Does Well

The dual-SIM auto-carrier-detection is the standout feature. Most cellular trail cameras force you to pick a carrier at setup and stick with it; the Flex-M scans both networks and picks the stronger signal automatically. For properties on the edge of coverage, this is the difference between a working camera and an expensive paperweight.

The free 100-photo plan covers genuine backyard use without ever paying Spypoint a dime in recurring fees. For users who set up a single camera and check it occasionally, that's the right price for the right volume.

Where It Falls Short

Daytime image quality is acceptable. Motion-blur tolerance is not. Verified-buyer reports consistently flag ghosting on fast-moving animals — squirrels, birds in flight, anything moving at speed. If your target species is slow-moving (deer, raccoons, opossums), this is a non-issue. If you're hoping to catch a hawk strike or a coyote chase, look elsewhere.

Flaws but not deal-breakers

720p video, not 1080p. A spec compromise that keeps the camera cheap and the cellular data costs low. For a phone-notification trail cam, video clarity is a secondary concern — the still photo is the primary alert.

Spypoint app interface is dated. Functional, occasionally clunky. You're checking it for "what showed up overnight?" not browsing photo galleries — the friction is real but minor.

AA batteries, not lithium pack. Eight AAs at a time gets expensive over years; rechargeable lithium would be cleaner. The trade-off is universal AA availability and no proprietary charger.

How It Compares

Versus the Stealth Cam DS4K Ultimate (our SD-card image-quality pick): the Stealth Cam is dramatically sharper but requires you to physically retrieve the SD card. Flex-M wins on convenience; DS4K wins on every image-quality axis.

Versus the Bushnell CelluCORE 20: similar price point, but the CelluCORE is locked to a single carrier at setup. If you don't know your property's cell coverage yet, Flex-M's auto-detect removes the gamble.

FAQ

Does the Flex-M work without a paid plan?

Yes. The free tier sends 100 photos per month with no card required. For occasional backyard use, that's enough indefinitely. Plans only become necessary at higher volumes or for video transfer.

Will it work on a property with weak cell signal?

Better than most. The dual-SIM auto-carrier-detection means it'll try both AT&T and Verizon and pick whichever is stronger. If neither carrier has any signal at your install location, no cellular trail cam will work — that's a connectivity problem, not a camera problem.

How does it compare to a non-cellular trail cam?

Non-cellular cams generally win on image quality at the same price tier (no cellular hardware to budget for) and require zero monthly considerations. The trade-off is you have to walk to the camera to see what it caught. If you're choosing convenience over image clarity, cellular is the call.
