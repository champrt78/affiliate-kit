# detailerpicks — Mockup Comparison (2026-05-18)

Four standalone HTML mockups for the detailerpicks home page + Best Car Wash Soap buying guide. Each one is a different aesthetic direction. All four solve the three flagged issues from the live site (centered body copy, single repeated Porsche hero, charcoal-everywhere monotony) by construction, not as patches.

Open each file in a browser. Each is self-contained, fonts load from Google Fonts, images load from Unsplash CDN with verified IDs.

- `detailerpicks-chrome-and-suds-mockup-2026-05-18.html` — Direction 1
- `detailerpicks-studio-white-editorial-mockup-2026-05-18.html` — Direction 2
- `detailerpicks-garage-warm-mockup-2026-05-18.html` — Direction 3
- `detailerpicks-sun-and-paint-mockup-2026-05-18.html` — Direction 4

Full-page screenshots of each are in `docs/playgrounds/screenshots/`.

## Competitive visual context (delta from the 2026-05-17 recon)

The earlier recon at `docs/competitive-recon-detailerpicks-2026-05-17.md` locked charcoal+electric-blue+brass on a premium-garage register, with chrome explicitly on the skip list. Ray's new directional input (lighter, clean, chrome reflections, suds, shine, sun on paint) supersedes that lock. The vertical-incumbent observations from that recon still stand:

- Adam's Polishes is the only competitor that gets premium-garage right, and they do it on near-black surfaces. Going lighter is a structural differentiator no incumbent has.
- AutoGeek and Detailed Image both wear a mid-2010s e-commerce skin. Anything genuinely contemporary beats them on first impression.
- The Drive runs a generic publication template with no distinctive visual identity. Distinctive is the floor, not the ceiling, in this vertical.
- Wirecutter remains the universal anchor but its red+cream+Georgia palette is a category convention; matching it too closely reads as me-too.

What changed: the four directions below all explore the territory the old recon ruled out. Three of the four are visibly lighter than charcoal on first glance. Only Garage Warm reaches back toward dark, and even there it goes warm (sodium-glow amber) rather than cold-tech.

## The four directions

### Direction 1 — Chrome & Suds (Showroom Light)

**One thing readers remember:** foam-as-texture, the wash-day photo carrying the brand on a near-white page.

**Visual thesis:** off-cream warm white surfaces, steel-blue accent, chrome-silver micro-details, dark-ink CTAs. Apple-product-page restraint applied to a working detail shop. The hero is a foam cascade against the bright body of the page, the manifesto is the only dark moment in the entire piece.

**Palette:** `#F4F2EE` off-cream, `#FBFAF7` paper bright, `#2C5E8C` steel blue, `#14181C` ink, `#BCBFC2` chrome.

**Typography:** Instrument Serif for display, Inter Tight for body. Instrument Serif is the differentiator — high-contrast classical serif (think New York Times masthead-italic, only thinner and more open), reads contemporary without dipping into Tiempos or Cooper territory.

**Strengths:**
- Hardest-working on the "lighter, cleaner" brief Ray put forward.
- The steel-blue accent is the same family as the old locked direction but lighter weight, so retained brand DNA with a different mood.
- Clear tonal stepping: paper-bright (hero) -> off-cream (lead) -> soft-cream (featured) -> ink (manifesto) -> off-cream (article) -> ink (Bottom Line border) -> soft-cream (picks) -> steel (FAQ). Six distinct tonal weights.
- Bottom Line callout is the only centered text in the article body, and it earns it: bordered card with top accent rule and the words "the bottom line" telegraphing the function.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Sits closer to a luxury car-product page than a car-care-publication. If detailerpicks needs to read as journalism more than commerce, this is on the commerce side of the line.
- Instrument Serif is currently free on Google Fonts but is a younger face than Fraunces. Long-term licensing is a non-issue (free) but the typeface itself carries less "publication-canonical" weight.

### Direction 2 — Studio White / Editorial

**One thing readers remember:** the print-magazine cover treatment. The hero plate, the issue number, the masthead — this site reads like an actual quarterly that decided to publish on the web.

**Visual thesis:** bone white background, oxblood-leather accent, gallery-framed photography with bordered plates and italic captions, masthead with issue numbering, manifesto as a deep oxblood spread. Cereal Magazine x Apartamento x Monocle.

**Palette:** `#F7F4ED` bone, `#FFFCF4` paper, `#6B2B2B` oxblood, `#B8553A` terra, `#C4933A` ochre, `#1A1612` deep warm black.

**Typography:** Newsreader for display (the most editorial-canonical optical serif on Google Fonts, used in italic for emphasis throughout), Inter Tight for body.

**Strengths:**
- The most differentiated from any incumbent. No car-detailing site reads like a print quarterly.
- Hardest stop on the "magazine, not blog, not commerce" register. Buyers who care about editorial credibility will recognize this immediately.
- Most internally cohesive palette. Oxblood + bone + ochre is a closed system; everything looks like it was art-directed together.
- Two distinct hero treatments: hero (microfiber/black hood, framed plate, portrait) and article (gallery-style 16:7 image, bordered, captioned).
- Most rigorous body type. Newsreader at 19px italic is a genuinely beautiful reading experience for the article body.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Furthest from "lighter and chrome" — the oxblood manifesto and the bone palette are warm and dark-paper, not bright. The "shine and suds" energy is muted.
- The manifesto panel reads "interiors magazine," which may be too tonally classical for car-detailing.
- Requires the strongest editorial copy to feel earned. If the body voice slips, the form feels overdressed.

### Direction 3 — Garage Warm (Sodium Light)

**One thing readers remember:** the warm garage glow on the hero, the sodium-amber light bouncing off a man waxing a car. Not a tech-blog dark mode. A real bay at end of day.

**Visual thesis:** warm concrete near-black for hero and FAQ, cream-paper inserts for reading sections, sodium amber as accent, rust/copper for emphasis. The site that the cold charcoal-and-cool-blue live site WANTED to be. Distinguishes itself from the old locked direction not by going lighter overall but by going warmer everywhere.

**Palette:** `#1F1B16` warm concrete, `#F2EAD9` cream paper, `#E69A35` amber, `#8C3D1F` rust, `#B86839` copper.

**Typography:** Fraunces (same family as mywildlifecam, different optical sizing), Inter Tight. Carries family resemblance to the sister site, then differentiates entirely through palette and atmosphere.

**Strengths:**
- Honors Ray's directional pull toward "warm" while still keeping enough drama to feel like detailing-vertical. This is the only direction where dark surfaces appear, and they appear warm.
- The hero image (man waxing car, sodium light) is a vertical-specific image no other direction earns. It IS the brand.
- The amber-underline detail in the manifesto ("by design") is a distinctive single-flourish moment that gives this direction a signature.
- Bottom Line callout against cream is the loudest of the four because the surrounding paper is bright and the box is dark.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Closest to the OLD locked direction in structure (hero on dark, content on light, accent color). Reads as "fix the existing site by warming it" rather than "new direction." That can be a feature (less to relearn for the brand) or a bug (less differentiation from where the site is now).
- The hero photo is currently a real-person-doing-work shot, which is excellent but limits to that genre of imagery long-term. If sourcing comparable images proves hard, the site loses its anchor.
- The Studio White and Sun & Paint mockups are visibly lighter on first scroll; this one only feels lighter inside the cream-paper article reads.

### Direction 4 — Sun & Paint (Outdoor Light)

**One thing readers remember:** the bright, outdoor-Saturday-afternoon energy of the orange-car hero with the cream plate cutting across the bottom. Reads confident and contemporary, not classical or magazine.

**Visual thesis:** sunwashed cream backdrop, sky-blue panel accents, safety-orange CTAs, asphalt-black manifesto with sun-spot warmth. The hero is a real outdoor shot, not a studio. The Bottom Line is a hard-edged yellow card with an offset black shadow (a single graphic-design moment in the article that earns its loudness). Wash day, no rain.

**Palette:** `#F4EFE3` cream, `#FBF8F0` paper bright, `#6AA6D6` sky, `#E85A1A` safety orange, `#1C1F22` asphalt, `#FFE08A` highlight.

**Typography:** Fraunces (italics heavy), Inter Tight (bold). Most contemporary feel of the four, leans on bold sans for utility moments (header pills, CTA buttons) and italic Fraunces for emotion (titles, captions).

**Strengths:**
- The brightest, most-energy direction. If Ray's directional pull is the truth, this is the one that gets furthest from "monotonous charcoal."
- The use-case pills under the hero are the cleanest visualization of the cross-brand-by-use-case story; readers see "ceramic coated / foam cannon / black paint / hard water" as the actual nav surface, not buried in body copy.
- The yellow Bottom Line callout with offset black shadow is the single most memorable graphic moment across all four mockups. It would carry standalone in social previews.
- Most palette variety: cream + sky + orange + yellow + asphalt is five distinct surface colors stepping through the page. The opposite of monotone.

**Tradeoffs:**
- The safety-orange CTA reads contemporary-confident but risks "ad-tech start-up" energy if not handled carefully (the offset-shadow card especially). Could be perceived as less serious than the editorial direction.
- Sky blue + cream + orange is the most distinctively "brand-system" palette of the four, which is good for memorability but constraining for satellites — the system is bigger than just neutrals + one accent.
- Fewer publication-style affordances (no bylines treatment, no issue numbering). Reads more digital-first.

## How they compare on the three flagged issues

| Issue | Direction 1 Chrome | Direction 2 Studio White | Direction 3 Garage Warm | Direction 4 Sun & Paint |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Body copy alignment | Left, lead drop cap, manifesto split-column left | Left, italic drop cap, masthead two-column left | Left, lead rule + sub-rubric, manifesto split-column left | Left, lead rubric + italic title, manifesto split-column left |
| Hero variation (3+ scenes) | Foam wash (home), foam wash close (featured), foam spray (article) | Microfiber on black hood (home), classic-car-reflection (featured), microfiber drag (article) | Man waxing in warm garage (home), mechanic workshop (featured), garage tools (article) | Orange paint/sun (home), red sedan on road (featured), black car + orange light (article) |
| Tonal section variation | Paper-bright -> cream -> soft-cream -> ink -> off-cream -> soft-cream -> steel-blue | Bone -> cream -> oxblood -> bone -> cream -> bone | Concrete -> paper warm -> paper soft -> concrete -> paper warm -> paper soft -> concrete | Cream -> sky blue -> asphalt -> cream -> paper -> sky shadow |

All four have at least 5 tonal steps per page. The current live site has 2 (charcoal everywhere with a slightly-deeper-charcoal review section). The monotony problem is solved structurally in every direction.

## Recommendation

If forced to pick one for production, **Direction 4 (Sun & Paint)** is the strongest signal-to-effort:

1. It commits hardest to Ray's directional input (lighter, sun, suds, shine).
2. It is the most differentiated from any incumbent in the vertical recon — nobody runs cream + sky-blue + safety-orange in car detailing.
3. The use-case pills under the hero solve a content-strategy problem (surfacing reader-segments) AND a visual problem (busy interesting hero strip) in one move.
4. The yellow Bottom Line card is the kind of distinctive graphic element that becomes a brand signature over time (same way mywildlifecam's brass rule + Fraunces italic is one).

**Direction 2 (Studio White / Editorial)** is the second strongest if the editorial credibility of the site is the highest priority, particularly for SEO/E-E-A-T trust signals. It is the most "we are a real publication" direction.

**Direction 3 (Garage Warm)** is the safest pick — it reads as a warm-and-corrected version of the existing site rather than a wholly new direction, which means less brand-relearn cost. It would be the right pick if Ray reviews and decides the existing brand DNA needs to be preserved.

**Direction 1 (Chrome & Suds)** is the prettiest but the least vertical-specific. It would work for any premium-affiliate site, which is both its strength (timeless, restrained) and its weakness (lacks the "this is car detailing" signature the other three carry).

Most likely fragment pick if Ray cherry-picks across them:
- Direction 4 hero treatment (sky-blue panel + safety-orange CTA + use-case pills)
- Direction 2 article body (Newsreader italic deck, bordered article plate, magazine-spread feel)
- Direction 4 Bottom Line callout (yellow card, offset shadow)
- Direction 1 or 4 manifesto (asphalt-on-cream or ink-on-cream — both work, neither is "go all the way dark for no reason")
- Avoid the Garage Warm hero unless reviewing photos and deciding "the warm bay shot" is genuinely the brand anchor
