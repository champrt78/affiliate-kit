## Who This Is For

This is for the home brewer who wants to start manual pour-over with the design the whole hobby is built around. The Hario V60 is the reference cone: most pour-over recipes, technique guides, and videos are written for it, so learning on a V60 means the entire library of how-to content applies directly to your brewer. It suits the person who wants a clean, bright cup and is willing to build a little technique to get it, and who would rather buy one complete starter set than assemble a dripper, server, and filters piece by piece.

One thing to set expectations on up front: the V60 is a brewer that rewards control. The single large hole and spiral ribs hand the timing and flow to you, which is the appeal, but it also means a gooseneck kettle and a scale are the two upgrades that most help a beginner get consistent. A precision gooseneck like the [Fellow Stagg EKG Pro](/reviews/fellow-stagg-ekg-electric-kettle-review) is the upgrade that most changes the pour. Neither is in the set, so think of this as the core of a pour-over kit rather than the whole thing.

## What It Does Well

- **It is the design the whole pour-over world is built around.** The V60 is the reference cone, so the recipes, ratios, and technique videos a beginner finds online are almost all written for this brewer. Learning on a V60 means everything you read applies to the cone in front of you, which lowers the friction of getting better.
- **It hands real control to the brewer.** The 60-degree cone, the spiral ribs, and the single large hole let you steer the flow with your pour. Slow down to extract more, speed up to extract less. That control is exactly what a pour-over enthusiast is after, and the V60 delivers it as well as anything at the price.
- **The set is a complete, inexpensive starting point.** Buying the ceramic dripper, a glass server, a scoop, and filters separately costs more than this bundle. As a first pour-over purchase it puts everything but the kettle and scale in one box, which is the friction-free way to begin.
- **It is small, simple, and does not plug in.** There is no motor, no boiler, and almost no footprint. It stores in a drawer and brews anywhere there is hot water, which suits both a tight kitchen and travel. For a brewer this capable, the simplicity is part of the appeal.

## Where It Falls Short

- **It rewards technique, so a sloppy pour shows.** The same control that makes the V60 great makes it less forgiving than a flat-bottom or immersion brewer. A rushed or uneven pour produces a worse cup. Beginners usually need a few brews, and ideally a gooseneck and a scale, to get consistent. The learning curve is the cost of the control.
- **It uses paper filters every brew.** The V60 takes Hario's cone filters as an ongoing consumable. The set includes a starter pack, but you will rebuy them, and the cone shape is specific to the V60. Keep a spare pack on hand and factor the filters into the running cost.
- **The best results need gear beyond the set.** A gooseneck kettle for a controlled stream and a scale for a repeatable ratio are near-essential to get the most from the cone, and neither is included. The true cost of brewing the V60 at its best is the set plus those two upgrades.
- **Ceramic and glass can break.** The ceramic dripper retains heat and feels substantial, which owners like, but it and the glass server are more fragile than the plastic or metal versions of the same cone. A drop is less forgiving here than on a plastic V60.

## How It Compares

The V60 is the **control-and-clarity** manual brewer rather than the most forgiving one. A few useful reference points:

**V60 vs a flat-bottom brewer.** Flat-bottom cones like the Kalita-style brewers spread the water over a wider, flatter bed, which makes the brew more even and more forgiving of an imperfect pour. The V60's single-hole cone gives more control and a brighter cup but asks more of the brewer. Pick the V60 if you want control and are willing to learn. Pick a flat-bottom brewer if you want consistency with less technique.

**V60 vs an immersion brewer.** An immersion device like a French press or a steep-and-release brewer soaks the grounds rather than pouring through them, which is about as forgiving as manual coffee gets and needs no pour technique at all. The V60 trades that forgiveness for a cleaner, more controllable cup. If hands-off simplicity matters more than control, immersion is the easier path.

**Ceramic V60 vs plastic, glass, or metal.** All four are the same 60-degree cone. This set uses the ceramic dripper, which holds heat well and feels substantial on a counter. Plastic is cheaper, lighter, and the best travel option. Glass matches the server's look. Metal is the toughest. The cone's behavior is the same across materials, so the choice is about heat retention, durability, and feel rather than the brew itself.

## Hario V60 02 vs 01 vs 03: which size should you buy?

This is a common point of confusion, so here is the plain answer. The number is the size of the cone, not a model generation. The **01** is the small cone aimed at single cups. The **02** reviewed here is the mid-size, suited to roughly 1 to 4 cups, and it is the standard most home brewers buy. The **03** is the large cone for bigger batches. Crucially, the filters are size-specific, so an 02 dripper takes 02 filters.

For almost everyone starting out, the 02 is the right size. It covers a single mug and scales up to a small batch for two, and because it is the most common size, it is the one most recipes assume and the easiest to find filters for. Step down to the 01 only if you exclusively brew one small cup at a time, and up to the 03 only if you regularly brew for a group.
