## Who This Is For

This is for the first-time espresso buyer with a small counter who wants real semi-automatic espresso rather than pods or capsules. The Bambino is built around the constraint most beginner machines ignore: width. At about 7.7 inches it fits where a wider machine will not, and it pairs that slim body with a 54mm commercial-style portafilter and a heater that is ready in seconds. It suits the person upgrading from a pod machine who is ready to learn to dial in a shot, and who either already owns a burr grinder or is prepared to buy one.

One thing to settle up front: this review covers the base Bambino BES450 with the manual steam wand, not the Bambino Plus with automatic milk texturing. If hands-off lattes are the goal, the Plus is the model to look at. If you want to learn to steam milk yourself and keep the price down, the base Bambino is the right one.

## What It Does Well

- **The fast heat-up is the headline, and it lands.** Breville's ThermoJet system is rated to reach extraction temperature in about 3 seconds. For a daily-driver machine that is the difference between brewing on impulse and waiting out a warm-up, and it is the feature owners single out most.
- **The footprint is genuinely small without giving up real espresso.** At about 7.7 inches wide the Bambino is the narrowest machine Breville sells, and it still uses a 54mm commercial-style portafilter rather than a shrunken proprietary basket. That combination is the reason it fits a tight counter while still letting you learn to dial in a shot.
- **It uses a real portafilter and a 15 bar pump.** The 54mm portafilter, 15 bar Italian pump, and low-pressure pre-infusion are the structural pieces of a proper semi-automatic, not a pod system dressed up. For a buyer who wants to learn the craft, the Bambino gives them the right tools to learn on.
- **It is positioned as the value entry to the Breville line.** Breville sells the Bambino below its grinder-included and larger machines, and across owner reviews the recurring theme is that it delivers cafe-style shots for the price and the size. For the small-counter beginner, that value framing is the draw.

## Where It Falls Short

- **There is no grinder, and the real cost reflects that.** The Bambino brews only. Good espresso needs a burr grinder, so the honest price is the machine plus a grinder. The [Baratza Encore ESP](/reviews/baratza-encore-esp) is the entry grinder purpose-tuned to reach espresso fineness that pairs naturally with a machine like this. Breville steers all-in-one buyers to the Barista Express for a reason, and a Bambino fed pre-ground coffee will not show what it can do.
- **The water tank and drip tray are small.** The slim body that sells the machine is the same body that limits tank and tray capacity. Fine for a solo or two-person routine, tighter when you are making drinks for a group.
- **The base model steams milk manually.** The BES450 has a manual steam wand. That is a feature for the buyer who wants to learn latte art and a limitation for the buyer who expected one-touch milk. The Bambino Plus is the model that automates it.
- **It is built to a price.** Owner reports and independent coverage note plastic in parts of the housing rather than the full metal of pricier machines. That trade-off is what keeps the price where it is, and it sits in the Build Quality score rather than out of sight.

## How It Compares

The Bambino is the **small-footprint value entry** in Breville's espresso line rather than the do-everything machine. A few useful reference points:

**Bambino BES450 vs Bambino Plus (BES500).** Same slim body, same fast heater. The Plus adds automatic milk texturing that steams to a set temperature and texture on a button press, plus a couple of extras, at a higher price. Pick the base Bambino if you want to learn to steam and save money. Pick the Plus if hands-off milk is the priority.

**Bambino vs Barista Express.** The Barista Express builds a conical burr grinder into the machine and adds a larger body and tank. It is the all-in-one for a buyer who does not want a separate grinder on the counter, at a meaningfully higher price and a larger footprint. Pick the Bambino when counter space is tight and you are happy to run a separate grinder. Pick the Barista Express when you want one machine to do everything and have the room for it.

**Bambino vs a pod machine.** A capsule machine is faster and tidier but locks you into pods and a fixed shot. The Bambino asks more of you, a grinder and the patience to dial in, and gives back real control over the cup. If the appeal of espresso at home is the craft and the freedom to use any beans, the Bambino is the side of that line to be on.

**Bambino vs the Gaggia Classic Evo Pro.** The other machine beginners cross-shop trades the Bambino's instant heat and small footprint for a metal body, a 58mm commercial portafilter, and a higher ceiling you can keep modding. We put the two side by side in our [Breville Bambino vs Gaggia Classic Evo Pro comparison](/comparisons/breville-bambino-vs-gaggia-classic-evo-pro).

## Breville Bambino vs Bambino Plus: which one should a beginner buy?

This is the question most searches on the Bambino are really asking, so here is the plain answer. The two machines share the things that make a Bambino a Bambino: the slim roughly 7.7-inch footprint, the ThermoJet heater rated at about a 3-second warm-up, the 54mm portafilter, and the 15 bar pump. The difference is milk. The base BES450 reviewed here has a **manual steam wand**, so you learn to texture milk yourself. The Bambino Plus adds **automatic milk texturing** that steams to a temperature and texture you set, plus a few extra conveniences, for more money.

So the choice comes down to one question: do you want to learn to steam milk, or do you want the machine to do it? If you are excited about the craft and want to keep the price down, the base Bambino is the right call and the manual wand is a feature, not a compromise. If you mainly want consistent lattes without practicing milk texturing, the Plus earns the upcharge. Either way the espresso side of the machine is the same, and either way you will want a separate burr grinder to get the best out of it. A calibrated tamper like the [Normcore spring tamper](/reviews/normcore-spring-tamper-review) is a common early upgrade for a machine you dial in by hand, and our guide to the [best espresso machine for beginners](/buyers-guides/best-espresso-machine-for-beginners) places the Bambino against the rest of the entry field.
