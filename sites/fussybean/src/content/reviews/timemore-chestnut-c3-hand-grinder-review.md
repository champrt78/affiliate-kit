## Who This Is For

This is for the brewer who wants a real burr grinder with a metal body and a CNC conical burr for well under the price of a benchtop electric. The Timemore Chestnut C3 is the budget hand grinder reviewers keep pointing to as the one that punches above its tier. It suits the person who brews pour-over, drip, French press, or AeroPress and wants a grinder tuned across that filter range, whether as a first real grinder or a compact second one.

One thing to settle up front: the standard C3 can reach espresso fineness, but it is tuned across a wide range rather than built around espresso. For filter coffee it is squarely in its element. If espresso is your main brew, Timemore sells a C3 ESP variant retuned with finer steps for it, and a dedicated espresso grinder is the precise tool. For everything in the filter range, the C3 delivers burr quality that owners say belies its price.

## What It Does Well

- **It puts a CNC metal burr in a budget grinder.** The C3 uses a CNC stainless conical burr in a metal body, and the recurring theme across owner reviews and independent coverage is that the build and grind quality outclass the price. For a first real grinder, that quality-per-dollar is the whole reason it gets recommended.
- **It is tuned for the brews most home users make.** Pour-over, drip, French press, and AeroPress all sit in the filter range the C3 is built around. For the buyer brewing those, the grinder is matched to the job rather than stretched to cover a range it was not designed for.
- **The numbered adjustment is repeatable.** Each setting is a number you can write down and return to, so dialing in a brew once means you can hit it again. For a brewer who wants consistency without re-guessing the grind each morning, that repeatability is the practical advantage over feel-based adjustment.
- **Double-bearing positioning steadies the burr.** Timemore lists a double bearing design that holds the burr aligned during grinding, which is part of why owners describe the grind as consistent for the price. It is a build detail that shows up in the cup as fewer stray fines and a steadier result.

## Where It Falls Short

- **Espresso is reachable, not the strong suit.** The standard C3 can grind fine enough for espresso, but the precise, repeatable fine steps espresso wants are better served by a dedicated espresso grinder or Timemore's C3 ESP variant. For filter coffee the C3 shines. For daily espresso it is a stretch.
- **Capacity is small, by design.** The 25g hopper holds about a single brew's worth of beans. Fine for one or two cups, tedious for grinding a batch for a group, where a larger grinder makes more sense.
- **It is manual effort every cup.** Hand grinding takes time and a little arm work. Quiet-grind seekers and small-space brewers see that as a fair trade. Anyone who wants to press a button and walk away will tire of the daily crank, and an electric grinder is the fix.
- **Internal adjustment is precise but slower to change.** The numbered setting is repeatable, which is its strength, but it is changed at the burr rather than via a quick external dial. For a brewer who stays at one setting it does not matter. For someone hopping between brew methods, an external-adjustment grinder is faster to move around.

## How It Compares

The C3 is the **budget-quality** hand grinder rather than the do-everything one. A few useful reference points:

**C3 vs an entry electric burr grinder.** An electric burr grinder grinds at the push of a button and holds a larger hopper, which suits high volume and anyone who does not want to crank by hand. The C3 trades that convenience for a fraction of the size and price, near-silent operation, and no power needed. Pick the C3 for value, small spaces, and a quiet grind. Pick an electric when volume and hands-off convenience matter most.

**C3 vs a pocket travel hand grinder.** A travel-first grinder built to nest inside an AeroPress optimizes for the smallest possible packed size, like the [1Zpresso Q2](/reviews/1zpresso-q2-hand-grinder-review). The C3 is a little larger and aimed at being an everyday home grinder at a budget price rather than a pure travel tool. If maximum packability is the goal, a dedicated travel grinder fits better. If a value everyday grinder is the goal, the C3 is the one reviewers point to.

**C3 vs a dedicated espresso grinder.** An espresso grinder is built to grind fine and consistent with the precise fine-step adjustment espresso needs. The standard C3 is built for the filter range and reaches espresso only as a stretch. If you pull espresso, that is the grinder to buy, or Timemore's C3 ESP, and the standard C3 is the complement for filter coffee.

## Timemore C3 vs C3 ESP vs C3S vs C3 Pro: which Chestnut should you buy?

A common point of confusion is that Timemore sells several grinders with close names, so here is the plain framing. The standard **C3** reviewed here is the budget filter-range grinder: a 25g capacity, a CNC stainless conical burr, numbered internal adjustment, and a fixed handle. The **C3 ESP** is the variant retuned with finer steps so more of the range sits in the espresso zone. The **C3S** and **C3 Pro** variants step up with changes like a different burr coating or a foldable handle for packing.

For the reader this review targets, a budget-minded brewer who wants real burr quality for pour-over, drip, French press, and AeroPress, the standard C3 is the right pick in the family. Step to the C3 ESP if espresso is your main brew, and to a C3S or C3 Pro only if you specifically want the upgraded burr or the foldable handle. Because the model names are close and Timemore updates the line over time, confirm the exact burr, capacity, adjustment style, and intended brew range on the listing before buying.
