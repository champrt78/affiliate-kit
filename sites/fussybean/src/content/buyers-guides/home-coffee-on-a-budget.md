## Is making coffee at home actually cheaper than a cafe?

The pitch for home coffee is usually some version of "you will save so much money," with no numbers attached. Here are the numbers, attributed.

Published 2025 cost comparisons put the average daily cafe habit near $1,100 a year, which works out to roughly 275 drinks at a little over three dollars each. If your order is a six to eight dollar specialty drink, the same comparisons push that figure past $1,500 a year.

Brewing the same coffee at home lands in a very different place. Across those comparisons, a home-brewed cup comes in under fifty cents and the at-home drinker near $85 a year, against roughly $985 a year for the person buying out. The per-cup gap is consistently two to four dollars in home's favor. These figures are drawn from published 2025 cost write-ups (Apartment List, Low Key Coffee Snobs, 42 Days Coffee, and 247 Calculator), and we state them as ranges because the exact numbers move with your habits.

A fair caveat: those are averages, and your mileage moves with bean prices, how much milk you pour, and what your local cafe charges. The direction does not change, though. Home coffee is dramatically cheaper to run. The only real question is the up-front cost of the gear, which is what the rest of this guide is about.

## The cheap-start kit

You do not need an espresso machine to drink good coffee at home, and starting with one is the most common way to overspend before you know what you like. The setup below is four pieces, and it is deliberately a kit rather than a ranking. Each piece does one job.

1. **A manual burr grinder.** The single most important purchase. A burr grinder crushes beans to a consistent size, which is what lets coffee extract evenly instead of brewing bitter and sour at once. Going manual skips the motor, which is most of the cost of an electric. This is the piece to buy first, and our guide to the [best coffee grinder for beginners](/buyers-guides/best-coffee-grinder-for-beginners) covers the hand grinders that fit a budget start.
2. **A brewer.** A French press is the forgiving, no-filter, no-electricity starting point. Coarse grind, hot water, four minutes, press. An AeroPress is the common alternative if you brew a single cup at a time. Our [AeroPress vs French Press comparison](/comparisons/aeropress-vs-french-press) walks the trade between them.
3. **A kettle.** Optional at the start. Any kettle or a pot off the stove will brew a French press. A variable-temperature gooseneck becomes worth it specifically once you want pour-over, where pour control and water temperature start to change the cup.
4. **A scale.** The quiet piece that makes the rest repeatable. Weighing your dose and water instead of guessing by scoops removes the biggest source of cup-to-cup inconsistency, and a basic one costs less than a week of cafe lattes.

The full four-piece kit above totaled about $225 as observed on 2026-05-30. A leaner press-only version (grinder, press, scale, no gooseneck) runs closer to $125. Both are below a single month of a daily cafe habit.

All four picks were checked for stock on their Amazon listings on 2026-05-30, and all four showed an active add-to-cart buybox at that time. Prices drift, so confirm the current figure on each product page before buying.

## The cheapest good coffee setup for one person

If you are brewing for one and tired of pods, this kit scales down cleanly to a single-serve setup. The two pieces that matter most for taste, a manual burr grinder and a brewer, are also the two that suit a solo drinker best. The Timemore Chestnut C2S grinds 25g at a time, enough for a generous single cup, and a hand grinder makes sense for one person precisely because you are not paying for a motor you barely use.

For the brewer, the French press here batches up to eight cups, which is more than a solo drinker needs day to day, so a single-cup brewer like an AeroPress is the common swap at a similar price. Both brew with coarse grounds, hot water, and a timer, with no paper to restock on the press. Add the scale to keep each cup repeatable, and you have a genuinely good single-serve setup for well under the cost of a month of cafe runs. The gooseneck kettle stays optional until pour-over becomes the goal, the same as it is for the full kit. For one person sick of pods, that grinder-plus-brewer core is the honest answer, no machine required.

## What to upgrade first

The order you spend matters as much as the total. If the budget is tight, build the kit in stages and let each piece prove itself before the next one.

**Buy the grinder first.** It does more for the cup than anything else, and a good grind makes even a cheap brewer taste better. Pre-ground coffee goes stale fast and is ground for one method, so a grinder is also where freshness lives.

**Add the brewer and scale together.** They are cheap, and they work as a pair. The brewer makes the coffee. The scale makes the recipe repeatable so you can actually improve it instead of guessing.

**Defer the kettle the longest.** This is the one piece where cheap and good genuinely diverge, and it is the one you need least to get started. A French press is happy with water from anything. Add a variable-temperature gooseneck when, and only when, pour-over becomes the goal. If you want pour-over control for less, a plain stovetop gooseneck with no electronics costs a fraction of an electric one. You give up the temperature presets and pour once the water comes off the boil.

The point of staging the buy is that you spend the least money on the things that matter most and defer the rest until you know you want them.

## Cost per cup, and when the kit pays for itself

Here is the part that makes the up-front gear cost easy to swallow.

Take the full $225 kit and a cafe habit averaging around $1,100 a year from the cost comparisons above. At roughly two to four dollars saved per cup, one cup a day, the kit pays for itself in the neighborhood of ten weeks. The leaner $125 press-only start pays back faster. After that break-even point, the ongoing cost is mostly just beans, and a home-brewed cup runs under fifty cents in the figures we cite.

Ongoing cost is also where the brewer choice quietly shows up. The French press uses a reusable steel mesh filter, so there is nothing to buy again. Pour-over uses paper filters, a small recurring expense. For the lowest possible per-cup math, the press wins. Pour-over trades a little ongoing cost for a cleaner cup and more control. Neither moves the big picture much against a four-dollar cafe drink.
