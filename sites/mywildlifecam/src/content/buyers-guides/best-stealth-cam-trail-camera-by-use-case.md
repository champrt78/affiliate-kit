## The doctrine splits, in plain language

Four design decisions divide this lineup. Knowing which side of each split matters to your deployment points to your pick.

- **Cellular vs SD-card.** The primary axis. Cellular models (DS4K Transmit, Deceptor MAX 2.0, Revolver Pro 2.0, Fusion MAX 2.0) deliver photos to the Command Pro app over AT&T or Verizon cellular networks and require a monthly subscription ($5 to $20 a month per stealthcam.com data-plan pricing). SD-card models (DS4K Ultimate, QS24, GMAX32) store photos to a removable SD card and require physical retrieval but carry no monthly fee. For backyard or close-to-house deployments where retrieval is a 5-minute walk, SD-card is the cost-rational pick. For back-of-property or remote-woodlot deployments where retrieval is a hike, cellular pays for itself in time saved. Some US states restrict cellular trail cameras for game-management purposes. Bowhunter's coverage flagged Colorado as one example. Check local regulations before buying a cellular model if you are deploying near hunting land.
- **True 940nm no-glow vs 850nm low-glow IR.** The covert-deployment axis. True 940nm no-glow LEDs do not produce a visible red glow at night. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 (cellular) and the GMAX32 (non-cellular) carry true 940nm no-glow IR per the stealthcam.com product pages. The DS4K Ultimate and DS4K Transmit use 850nm low-glow IR, which produces a faint visible red glow at night. The Revolver Pro 2.0 uses standard red-glow IR per TrailCamPro's review. For wildlife monitoring on a public-trail-adjacent property, on a fenced perimeter, or anywhere a visible red dot at night is a concern, the no-glow models are the right pick. The DS4K Transmit is the trap here: it markets as the cellular flagship but is not the covert cellular pick. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 is.
- **4K vs 1440P vs 1080P video.** Only the DS4K Ultimate and DS4K Transmit deliver true 4K video. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 and Revolver Pro 2.0 deliver 1440P. The Fusion MAX 2.0 tops out at 1080P. For wildlife documentation where pulling video stills is part of the workflow, 4K matters and the DS4K models lead. For remote cellular monitoring where photos (not video) are the transmission unit, 4K becomes a moot point: cellular pipelines transmit images, and the resolution gain is invisible at the app level.
- **Command Pro app reliability.** The cellular Stealth Cam models share the in-house Command Pro app. Multiple support forums (Rokslide, NC Hunt and Fish, JustAnswer) document recurring connectivity complaints: app not loading, no-signal errors, Bluetooth pairing failures, the need to reinstall to recover. The stealthcam.com FAQ page lists app-loading failure as a known support case. The 3.0 firmware cycle is positioned to improve the cellular pipeline (dual-core simultaneous capture-plus-upload), but the improvement has not been independently validated as of May 2026. Practical buyer advice: keep the app updated, pair over Bluetooth fresh after each firmware update, reinstall before contacting support. Browning and Moultrie run similar in-house apps with similar complaint patterns. This is not a Stealth-Cam-only issue, but it is real friction.

## DS4K Ultimate vs Transmit vs Max: which Stealth Cam DS4K?

The DS4K name and the "Max" naming both span more than one Stealth Cam model, and buyers routinely arrive confused about which is which. The split is clean once you separate connectivity from the imaging stack.

The **DS4K Ultimate** is the SD-card 4K flagship. Per stealthcam.com, 32MP photos, 4K at 30 FPS, a 0.2-second trigger, 100 ft detection, and 850nm low-glow IR, with no cellular and no monthly fee. You walk out and pull the card. Our [Stealth Cam DS4K Ultimate review](/reviews/stealth-cam-ds4k-ultimate-review) digs into the image quality that defines it, and two head-to-heads place it against the no-subscription field: [Browning Strike Force vs Stealth Cam DS4K](/comparisons/browning-strike-force-vs-stealth-cam) on speed versus image quality, and [GardePro E5 vs Stealth Cam DS4K](/comparisons/gardepro-e5-vs-stealth-cam-ds4k) on budget versus image quality.

The **DS4K Transmit** carries that same 32MP and 4K-at-30-FPS imaging stack and adds dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon cellular plus GPS, so photos reach your phone. It runs a $5 to $20 a month Command Pro plan, and it uses the same 850nm low-glow IR as the Ultimate, not no-glow. So it is the cellular 4K pick, not the covert pick.

The **Max** names point to Stealth Cam's cellular line rather than a DS4K variant. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 is the no-glow covert cellular camera (940nm no-glow IR, 40MP, dual-SIM), and the Fusion MAX 2.0 is the budget cellular pick (36MP, 1080P, dual-SIM). If a search for "DS4K Max" sent you here, the camera you likely want is one of those two cellular MAX models, covered in their own entries above.

The short version: pick the DS4K Ultimate for the best image with no subscription, the DS4K Transmit for that imaging delivered to your phone, the Deceptor MAX 2.0 for covert no-glow cellular, and the Fusion MAX 2.0 for budget cellular.

## Best Stealth Cam for Covert Property Monitoring

For a property owner who wants a camera that does not announce itself at night, the deciding spec is true 940nm no-glow IR. A no-glow camera throws no visible red glow when it fires, so it sits quietly on a post, a fence line, or a neighbor-visible property edge without a faint red dot giving it away in the dark. Two picks in this lineup carry true 940nm no-glow IR per the stealthcam.com product pages.

The **Deceptor MAX 2.0** is the covert cellular pick. True 940nm no-glow IR, dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon, 40MP photos, and a TrailCamPro-measured 8.4-month battery life in photo mode with a 96 of 100 detection score. For an out-of-reach spot where you want captures pushed to your phone without the camera being seen, it is the strongest covert option here.

The **GMAX32** is the covert SD-card pick under $100. 42 940nm no-glow LEDs in a matte no-glare array, 32MP photos, and an 80 ft detection range, with no cellular and no monthly fee. For a reachable property edge where you can pull the card yourself, it delivers the no-glow covert profile without the cellular subscription.

The trap worth flagging again: the DS4K Transmit markets as the cellular flagship but uses 850nm low-glow IR, which shows a faint visible glow at night. For covert monitoring specifically, the Deceptor MAX 2.0 or the GMAX32 are the right picks, not the DS4K Transmit. The honest limit on all of these is that a trail camera records on motion rather than streaming live, so for real-time monitoring a dedicated outdoor security camera is the right category instead.
