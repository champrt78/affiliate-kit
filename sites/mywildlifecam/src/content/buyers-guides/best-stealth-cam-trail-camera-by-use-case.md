## Who This Is For

Seven Stealth Cam reader segments map to the seven picks in this guide. The lineup splits across two main format axes: cellular vs SD-card, and true 940nm no-glow vs low-glow 850nm IR.

- **The image-quality buyer with no cellular subscription appetite.** The **Stealth Cam DS4K Ultimate** is the non-cellular 4K flagship. 32MP, 4K at 30 FPS, 0.2-second trigger, 100 ft detection per the stealthcam.com product page. No app, no monthly bill, walk out for the SD card. Existing single-product review on this site at `/reviews/stealth-cam-ds4k-ultimate-review`.
- **The cellular 4K buyer who does not need covert IR.** The **Stealth Cam DS4K Transmit** is the cellular 4K flagship. Same 32MP and 4K imaging as the DS4K Ultimate, plus dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon with Automatic Network Coverage, plus built-in GPS. Important note: the DS4K Transmit uses 850nm low-glow IR per the stealthcam.com product page, not 940nm no-glow. It is the cellular 4K pick, not the covert cellular pick.
- **The covert-deployment cellular buyer.** The **Stealth Cam Deceptor MAX 2.0** is the true no-glow covert cellular pick. 940nm IR LEDs, 40MP interpolated, 1440P video, 0.22-second photo trigger per TrailCamPro's measured testing. TrailCamPro scored it 89/100. 16GB internal memory, no SD card required. Battery life of 8.4 months in photo mode per TrailCamPro's measured testing.
- **The open-area 360-degree buyer.** The **Stealth Cam Revolver Pro 2.0 360** captures a full 360-degree panoramic field with 1-6 selectable PIR zones. TrailCamPro scored it 92/100 in open-area testing. Important caveat: TrailCamPro's review specifically flagged significant false triggers from vegetation in wooded deployments. The Revolver is an open-area-only pick. Food plots, clearings, driveways, fenced backyards. Not woods, not brush.
- **The budget cellular buyer and gift buyer.** The **Stealth Cam Fusion MAX 2.0** sits in the entry-cellular slot. 36MP, 1080P, dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon, 16GB internal memory. Pricing on stealthcam.com runs $89.99 to $149.99 depending on bundle. For the brand-recognized cellular gift purchase under $150, this is the pick.
- **The first-camera or zero-subscription gift buyer.** The **Stealth Cam QS24** is the entry-level non-cellular pick. $79.99 standard or $119.99 as the No-Glo Combo bundle (adds SD card and security box). 24MP, 720P video, 80 ft detection, LoGlo IR. The trigger speed of 0.8 seconds is slower than the higher tiers, which is fine for backyard distance work where animals move slowly past the camera.
- **The non-cellular covert buyer who wants true no-glow without flagship pricing.** The **Stealth Cam GMAX32** is the non-cellular no-glow pick. 42 940nm IR LEDs with a matte No-Glare PIR and IR array to eliminate glare. 32MP, 1080P, 0.4-second trigger, 80 ft detection. Priced around $71.75 on Amazon at time of research, well below the DS4K Ultimate. For SD-card-only covert deployments on a property edge or a public-trail-adjacent woodlot.

## The picks, ranked by reader-segment fit

The ranking in this guide is not about which Stealth Cam is best in absolute terms (TrailCamPro's scores cluster in the 85-to-92 range across the cellular lineup, which is a tight band). The ranking reflects which reader segment each model fits best, based on documented specs, TrailCamPro's third-party measured testing, and credible forum signal from Rokslide and other communities.

### 1. Stealth Cam DS4K Ultimate: best for the image-quality non-cellular buyer

The DS4K Ultimate is the non-cellular 4K flagship in the lineup. Per the stealthcam.com product page, the camera captures 32MP photos and 4K video at 30 FPS, with a 0.2-second trigger speed and 100 ft detection range. Per Bowhunter's editorial coverage, the camera also records 1440P at 30 FPS as a secondary video option. IR is 850nm low-glow infrared (Bowhunter described it as "Low Glow infrared flash range"). Power is 8 AA batteries; SD card support up to 32GB.

The pitch is straightforward: 4K image quality plus no monthly subscription. No app to fight with, no carrier handshake to debug, no scheduled tier upgrade to figure out. Walk out, swap the SD card, walk back. For property owners with a camera deployment close enough to the house that retrieval is not annoying, this is the maximum-image-quality pick in the Stealth Cam lineup.

Cross-link: read the full [Stealth Cam DS4K Ultimate review](/reviews/stealth-cam-ds4k-ultimate-review) for the deeper spec breakdown, owner-review pattern analysis, and the trade-offs that matter for backyard deployments.

Shelf-life flag worth knowing: Stealth Cam announced the **Spectre 4K Pro** in January 2026 as part of the 3.0 series. It is positioned as the successor to the DS4K Ultimate in the non-cellular 4K slot. As of May 2026, the Spectre 4K Pro has not been independently reviewed by TrailCamPro and confirmed pricing was not published. The DS4K Ultimate remains the buyable, reviewed, and priced pick. If the Spectre 4K Pro ships and earns comparable or better third-party scores before the DS4K Ultimate goes out of stock, the DS4K Ultimate reframes as the proven older model at a lower price.

### 2. Stealth Cam DS4K Transmit: best for cellular 4K without covert-IR requirements

The DS4K Transmit takes the DS4K Ultimate's imaging stack and bolts a cellular pipeline onto it. Per the stealthcam.com product page, the camera carries the same 32MP sensor and 4K-at-30-FPS video as the DS4K Ultimate, with a 0.2-second trigger and 100 ft detection range. It adds dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon connectivity with Automatic Network Coverage per the spec sheet. It is the first GPS-enabled cellular model in the Stealth Cam lineup. Power is 12 AA batteries; SD card support up to 128GB.

The doctrine split worth flagging clearly: the DS4K Transmit uses 850nm LEDs (low-glow infrared), not 940nm (true no-glow), per the stealthcam.com product page. This matters because Stealth Cam markets the DS4K Transmit as the cellular flagship, which a covert-deployment buyer would reasonably assume includes no-glow IR. It does not. For wildlife monitoring on a publicly visible trail, on a property edge near neighbors, or in any context where a visible faint red glow at night is a concern, the Deceptor MAX 2.0 is the correct pick, not the DS4K Transmit.

Data plan pricing per stealthcam.com runs from $5 a month (600 photos) to $20 a month (unlimited photos). The DS4K Transmit sits above the Deceptor and Fusion cellular tier on imaging but does not have an announced 3.0 successor as of May 2026. The next-gen successor for the cellular 4K slot has not been formally named.

### 3. Stealth Cam Deceptor MAX 2.0: best for true 940nm no-glow covert cellular

The Deceptor MAX 2.0 is the no-glow covert pick in the Stealth Cam cellular lineup. TrailCamPro's measured testing (model STC-DCPTRX2) confirms 940nm no-glow IR, 40MP interpolated photos (selectable: 4/8/16/40MP), 1440P video, 0.22-second photo trigger, 0.52-second video trigger, 1.1-second photo recovery, 13.1-second video recovery, and 120 ft detection at a 59-degree angle. TrailCamPro's overall score: 89/100 (Picture Quality 86, Battery 90, Detection 96, Design 85).

The battery-life claim worth highlighting: TrailCamPro measured 8.4 months in photo mode and 5.8 months in video mode on standard AA batteries. The transmission speed for image upload is approximately 16.5 seconds per image. Internal storage is 16GB, no SD card required. Dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon with Automatic Network Coverage, per TrailCamPro. Battery options include FieldMAX lithium cartridges and solar panels.

For covert deployment, the true no-glow IR plus the 96/100 Detection score are the load-bearing specs. The camera does not produce a visible red glow at night. The PIR detection is among the strongest in the cellular tier per TrailCamPro's measured testing. Stealth Cam announced the Deceptor MAX 3.0 in January 2026; existing 2.0 owners will receive the AI features as a firmware update per the American Hunter announcement, which softens the shelf-life risk on this pick.

Price on stealthcam.com at time of research was $89.99 (down from $129.99), which is aggressive given the third-party scoring. The Amazon listing covers both single-unit and 2-pack configurations.

### 4. Stealth Cam Revolver Pro 2.0 360: best for open-area 360-degree coverage

The Revolver Pro 2.0 captures a full 360-degree panoramic field. Per TrailCamPro's measured testing, the camera offers 1-6 user-selectable PIR zones, 40MP interpolated photos, 1440P QHD video with audio, a 0.22-second photo trigger, 110 ft detection per PIR zone at a 52.2-degree angle, dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon cellular, 32GB internal memory (no SD card required), and a 15.4-month battery life in photo mode (4.2 months in video mode). The 2.0 generation added live-stream capability. TrailCamPro's overall score: 92/100.

Important caveat that the marketing does not advertise: TrailCamPro's review specifically flagged significant false triggers from vegetation in wooded or brush-heavy deployments when all 6 PIR zones are active. The "six cameras in one" framing holds up in open ground (food plots, driveways, clearings, fenced backyards). It breaks down in brush. For a property owner with a cleared deployment area, the Revolver Pro 2.0 is a uniquely capable cellular pick. For wooded property edges or trail-corridor monitoring, the Deceptor MAX 2.0 is the safer bet.

IR is standard red-glow infrared (not no-glow). For full-360-degree coverage with no visible night flash, no current Stealth Cam model fits. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 gives no-glow at a single point-of-aim; the Revolver Pro 2.0 gives 360-degree coverage with visible IR.

Price on stealthcam.com was $99.99 at time of research (down from $149.99). The Revolver PRO 3.0 was announced in January 2026; firmware update covers the AI features for existing 2.0 units per the American Hunter coverage.

### 5. Stealth Cam Fusion MAX 2.0: best for budget cellular and the brand-recognized gift purchase

The Fusion MAX 2.0 is the entry-cellular pick in the lineup. Per the stealthcam.com product page, 36MP photos, 1080P video, dual-SIM AT&T and Verizon with Automatic Network Coverage, 16GB internal memory, 100 ft detection range, and 0.35-to-0.4-second trigger speed depending on Fusion variant. Power is 16 AA batteries; SD card support is available but internal memory removes the requirement.

For the backyard property monitoring use case (cellular convenience, dual-SIM coverage on rural carrier-edge properties, app-driven photo retrieval), the Fusion MAX 2.0 hits the right price band. Standard pricing on stealthcam.com runs $89.99 single (sale) to $149.99 (full price), with 2-pack and 4-pack bundles available on Amazon. The cellular subscription is the same Command Pro tier as the higher cellular models ($5 to $20 a month per stealthcam.com).

For the gift buyer who wants a brand-recognized cellular trail cam under $150, this is the answer. For the property owner who wants the longest battery life and no glow IR, the Deceptor MAX 2.0 is the better pick at a similar price band. The Fusion MAX 2.0 trades that for broader brand recognition and a more familiar price point.

Stealth Cam announced the Fusion MAX 3.0 in January 2026 as part of the same firmware-update cycle. Existing 2.0 units pick up the AI features when the rollout completes per the American Hunter announcement.

### 6. Stealth Cam QS24: best for the first-camera or zero-subscription gift purchase

The QS24 is the entry-level non-cellular pick on the current lineup. Per the stealthcam.com product page, 24MP photos, 720P video at 30 FPS, 80 ft detection range, 0.8-second trigger speed, and LoGlo IR (Stealth Cam's term for low-glow infrared). Standard pricing on stealthcam.com is $79.99; the No-Glo Combo bundle at $119.99 adds an SD card and a security box.

The QS24 fits two specific segments cleanly. First-time trail-cam buyers who do not want to deal with the carrier-handshake or app-connectivity friction of a cellular model. Gift buyers who want the recipient to be able to use the camera the day it arrives, with no subscription decision required. The slower trigger speed (0.8 seconds) is fine for backyard deployments where animals walk past the camera rather than sprint across the field of view.

For the kids' woodlot project, the holiday-gift trail camera, or the property owner who wants a second SD-card camera as a budget supplement to a flagship deployment, the QS24 is the right pick. It is also the cheapest current Stealth Cam SKU, which makes it the lowest-stakes way to test whether trail cameras are something the recipient cares about before stepping up to a flagship or cellular model.

### 7. Stealth Cam GMAX32: best for non-cellular true 940nm no-glow under $100

The GMAX32 is the non-cellular no-glow pick. Per the stealthcam.com product page and the Amazon listing, the camera carries 42 940nm IR LEDs in a matte No-Glare PIR and IR array configuration, 32MP photos, 1080P video at 30 FPS, 0.4-second trigger speed, and 80 ft detection range. SD card support up to 32GB; power is 8 AA batteries. Amazon listing was active at the time of research at $71.75 per Canopy lookup, well below the DS4K Ultimate's $249.99 MSRP.

For the SD-card buyer who wants true no-glow IR without paying flagship money, the GMAX32 is the only current pick. The DS4K Ultimate is the higher-image-quality non-cellular pick, but it uses 850nm low-glow IR, not 940nm no-glow. For covert deployment scenarios (a public-trail-adjacent property edge, a fenced perimeter, a neighbor-visible camera location), the GMAX32 covers the gap that the DS4K Ultimate leaves open at the higher end.

One stock note: the stealthcam.com product page showed out-of-stock status for the GMAX32 at time of research, though the Amazon listing remained active with confirmed pricing. If the Amazon listing goes inactive before this guide refreshes, the closest replacement is the DS4K Ultimate (better image, no-glow gap) or the Deceptor MAX 2.0 if the buyer can accept the cellular subscription cost.

## The doctrine splits, in plain language

Four design decisions divide this lineup. Knowing which side of each split matters to your deployment points to your pick.

**1. Cellular vs SD-card.** The primary axis. Cellular models (DS4K Transmit, Deceptor MAX 2.0, Revolver Pro 2.0, Fusion MAX 2.0) deliver photos to the Command Pro app over AT&T or Verizon cellular networks and require a monthly subscription ($5 to $20 a month per stealthcam.com data-plan pricing). SD-card models (DS4K Ultimate, QS24, GMAX32) store photos to a removable SD card and require physical retrieval but carry no monthly fee. For backyard or close-to-house deployments where retrieval is a 5-minute walk, SD-card is the cost-rational pick. For back-of-property or remote-woodlot deployments where retrieval is a hike, cellular pays for itself in time saved. Some US states restrict cellular trail cameras for game-management purposes; Bowhunter's coverage flagged Colorado as one example. Check local regulations before buying a cellular model if you are deploying near hunting land.

**2. True 940nm no-glow vs 850nm low-glow IR.** The covert-deployment axis. True 940nm no-glow LEDs do not produce a visible red glow at night. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 (cellular) and the GMAX32 (non-cellular) carry true 940nm no-glow IR per the stealthcam.com product pages. The DS4K Ultimate and DS4K Transmit use 850nm low-glow IR, which produces a faint visible red glow at night. The Revolver Pro 2.0 uses standard red-glow IR per TrailCamPro's review. For wildlife monitoring on a public-trail-adjacent property, on a fenced perimeter, or anywhere a visible red dot at night is a concern, the no-glow models are the right pick. The DS4K Transmit is the trap here: it markets as the cellular flagship but is not the covert cellular pick. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 is.

**3. 4K vs 1440P vs 1080P video.** Only the DS4K Ultimate and DS4K Transmit deliver true 4K video. The Deceptor MAX 2.0 and Revolver Pro 2.0 deliver 1440P. The Fusion MAX 2.0 tops out at 1080P. For wildlife documentation where pulling video stills is part of the workflow, 4K matters and the DS4K models lead. For remote cellular monitoring where photos (not video) are the transmission unit, 4K becomes a moot point: cellular pipelines transmit images, and the resolution gain is invisible at the app level.

**4. Command Pro app reliability.** The cellular Stealth Cam models share the in-house Command Pro app. Multiple support forums (Rokslide, NC Hunt and Fish, JustAnswer) document recurring connectivity complaints: app not loading, no-signal errors, Bluetooth pairing failures, the need to reinstall to recover. The stealthcam.com FAQ page lists app-loading failure as a known support case. The 3.0 firmware cycle is positioned to improve the cellular pipeline (dual-core simultaneous capture-plus-upload), but the improvement has not been independently validated as of May 2026. Practical buyer advice: keep the app updated, pair over Bluetooth fresh after each firmware update, reinstall before contacting support. Browning and Moultrie run similar in-house apps with similar complaint patterns. This is not a Stealth-Cam-only issue, but it is real friction.
