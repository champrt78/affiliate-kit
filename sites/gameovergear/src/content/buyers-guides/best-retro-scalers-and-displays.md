## Converter vs upscaler vs FPGA line multiplier: what you are choosing

Getting a retro console onto a modern TV splits into a few approaches, and the differences matter more than the price tags.

- **Cheap composite converters.** The GANA box takes the soft composite signal almost every cartridge-era console outputs by default and turns it into HDMI. It is the lowest-friction and lowest-cost path, and per RetroRGB it is also the lowest-fidelity one, because composite is already the softest analog source and the converter adds its own processing. Treat it as a get-it-working step, not a quality endpoint.
- **Console-specific RGB cables.** The LEVELHIKE adapter is built for the SNES and Super Famicom and pulls the console's RGB signal rather than composite. My Life in Gaming has documented that RGB carries more picture information than composite or S-video, so a per-console RGB cable can look noticeably cleaner. The trade-off is that it serves one console family, so a multi-system setup needs several.
- **Inline plug-in upscalers.** The Marseille mClassic Retro Edition sits on the HDMI cable and applies a real-time processing pass, rated up to 1440p/60Hz, per Marseille. It is the most convenient way to add cleanup to an existing HDMI or component signal, with the understood limit that an inline post-processor cannot recover detail the console never output.
- **FPGA line multipliers.** The Kaico OSSC uses field-programmable gate arrays to line-multiply SCART, component and VGA sources with near-zero lag, per the listing and as documented by Modern Vintage Gamer and My Life in Gaming. It is the enthusiast tier among Amazon-stocked options, and it rewards a clean RGB source rather than rescuing raw composite.

There is no single correct approach. The right pick depends on what your console outputs (composite, RGB, component or SCART), how many systems you are feeding, and whether low cost, convenience, or signal fidelity matters most.

## RetroTINK 5X vs OSSC: which should you buy?

This is the defining comparison in retro AV, and the two devices solve the same problem in different ways. The **Kaico Edition OSSC**, the FPGA line multiplier in this guide at roughly $169.99, line-multiplies SCART, component, and VGA sources with near-zero lag, but it does little processing beyond that and is happiest with a clean RGB input.

The **RetroTINK-5X Pro sits a tier above it.** Per the RetroTINK shop and ConsoleMods, the 5X Pro is a custom-FPGA video processor priced at $325 that accepts composite, S-Video, component, RGB, and SCART, scales up to 1440p, and adds genuine image processing, with a Frame Lock mode rated at roughly 0.25 frames of lag. The practical split: the **OSSC is the cheaper, purist line multiplier** for someone who already has clean RGB and wants minimal latency, while the **RetroTINK 5X Pro is the more flexible processor** that also cleans up composite and S-Video and does more to the picture, at nearly double the price. The important availability note, as flagged above, is that the OSSC is on Amazon while the RetroTINK line sells direct through retrotink.com, so the OSSC is the high end of what this guide can link.

## Best budget upscaler vs premium: what you actually get

The price ladder in retro scaling buys two different things: **getting a picture at all, and then getting a clean one**. At the budget end, the roughly $14.99 GANA converter gets a composite-only console onto an HDMI TV, but per RetroRGB it is the **lowest-fidelity path**, because it starts from the softest analog signal and adds its own processing. The roughly $61 Marseille mClassic is the middle, a convenient inline cleanup pass on an existing HDMI or component signal, with the limit that it cannot recover detail the console never sent.

The premium tier, the FPGA OSSC here at roughly $169.99 and the direct-sale RetroTINK above it, is where you get **sharp, low-latency output**, but only if you feed it a clean RGB or component source. The honest rule is that **fidelity tracks the source signal as much as the box**: a premium scaler fed composite still looks soft, so spend up only once you can give the device a clean signal to work with.

## FAQ

**RetroTINK or OSSC for a modern 4K TV?** Both output HDMI that a 4K TV accepts, but they target different buyers. The **OSSC is the cheaper, near-zero-lag line multiplier**, best when you already have a clean RGB, component, or SCART source. The **RetroTINK-5X Pro**, per the RetroTINK shop, adds composite and S-Video input plus real image processing and scaling up to 1440p at roughly 0.25 frames of lag, for more flexibility at $325. The catch is availability: the OSSC is on Amazon, while the RetroTINK sells direct through retrotink.com. For a clean-RGB setup on a budget, the OSSC. For broader input support and more processing, the RetroTINK.

**Do I need a scaler or just a converter?** It depends on your console's output and how much picture quality you want. A **converter** like the GANA box is enough to get a composite-only console showing on an HDMI TV, but per RetroRGB it is the lowest-fidelity path. A **scaler or line multiplier** like the OSSC produces a sharper, lower-lag picture, but it rewards a clean RGB or component source and costs much more. If you just want it working, a converter. If you want it looking good and can feed a clean signal, a scaler.

**Cheapest way to get clean retro video on a flat panel?** The cheapest genuinely clean route is a **console-specific RGB cable** where one exists, like the roughly $29.99 LEVELHIKE for the SNES and Super Famicom, since pulling RGB at the source beats converting composite. For a console that only outputs composite, the roughly $14.99 GANA converter is the cheapest way to a picture, with the honest caveat that composite is the softest signal and no cheap box makes it sharp. Clean and cheap usually means feeding RGB, not upscaling composite after the fact.
