## What you are actually solving

A retro console and a modern TV **speak different languages**. Cartridge-era systems output analog video, most often the soft composite signal carried on a single yellow RCA plug, at a low resolution like 240p. A 4K flat panel expects a clean digital HDMI signal and has no analog input at all on most current models. Connecting the two is a **translation job**, and the quality of that translation is what separates a sharp picture from a soft, laggy, or no-sync mess.

There is a second problem hiding behind the first. Even once the TV accepts the signal, the picture can look wrong, stretched, blurry, or delayed, because the console's original output was designed for a CRT, not a sample-and-hold LCD. This guide walks the signal path in order and notes where each piece of gear fits.

## Connection types: composite vs RGB vs HDMI

The single biggest factor in picture quality is what kind of signal your console can output. From softest to sharpest, the common analog options are:

- **Composite (CVBS).** The yellow RCA plug. It bundles all the video information onto one wire, which is why it looks the softest and shows the most color bleed and dot crawl. Nearly every cartridge-era console can do composite, and many can do nothing better without modification. RetroRGB documents composite as the lowest-fidelity standard path.
- **S-Video.** Separates brightness from color onto two wires, a clear step up from composite, supported by some consoles such as the N64 and certain Genesis models.
- **RGB / component.** Carries the red, green and blue picture information on separate channels, the cleanest analog output a retro console can produce. My Life in Gaming has covered at length why RGB looks markedly sharper than composite. Some consoles output RGB natively over SCART or component. Others need a specific cable or an internal mod.

On the digital side, **HDMI** is what the TV wants. There are two ways to get there. A general converter box (like the GANA above) takes whatever analog signal the console outputs and turns it into HDMI. A console-specific cable (like the LEVELHIKE for SNES) taps a cleaner RGB signal at the source and outputs HDMI directly, which is why a per-console cable can beat a universal composite converter.

The practical rule: feed the cleanest signal your console can produce. A composite-only console is limited to a composite converter, but an RGB-capable console rewards a cable or device that pulls RGB rather than composite.

## The lag question

Input lag is the second thing people notice and the hardest to see in a spec sheet. Two separate sources add delay.

The first is the TV itself. Modern panels run image processing, motion smoothing, and noise reduction that buffer frames before display. Most TVs include a **Game Mode** picture setting that disables the heaviest of this processing. Enabling it is the single most effective lag fix and costs nothing. Check the TV's input or picture menu for it.

The second source is the converter. Any device that buffers a whole frame to scale or process it adds latency, and this is the technical distinction between two device classes. A scaler or converter that **frame-buffers adds measurable lag**. A **line multiplier**, such as an FPGA-based device, processes the signal line by line rather than frame by frame and is marketed as effectively zero-lag for that reason.

Modern Vintage Gamer and My Life in Gaming have both documented this difference in their analog-to-digital coverage. The inline mClassic in this guide is rated by Marseille as lag-free. Cheaper composite converters generally add some delay, which is one more reason RetroRGB frames them as a starting point rather than an endpoint.

If a setup feels laggy, check Game Mode first, then consider whether the converter in the chain is a frame-buffering type.

## Region lock: NTSC vs PAL

Region matters in two ways, and they are easy to confuse.

The first is the **console's cartridge lockout**, the system refusing to run a game from another region. That is a console-level restriction and a converter or cable does nothing to change it. It is solved at the cartridge or console level, outside the scope of a display setup.

The second is the **video standard**, NTSC (used in North America and Japan) versus PAL (used across much of Europe). These differ in resolution and refresh rate, historically about 60Hz for NTSC and 50Hz for PAL. A converter has to understand the incoming standard to output a stable picture. The GANA converter above lists support for both PAL and NTSC sources, which is why it can handle an imported console feeding a North American TV. If a converter only accepts one standard and the console outputs the other, the result is a rolling, no-sync, or wrong-speed picture. Match the converter's supported standards to the console's region before buying.

## Step by step

A reliable order of operations, working from the console outward:

1. **Identify the console's best output.** Check whether it can do composite only, S-video, or RGB/component. This decides which device in this guide fits.
2. **Pick the conversion path.** For a composite-only console, a universal converter like the GANA box. For an RGB-capable console with a dedicated cable available, such as the SNES, a console-specific RGB cable like the LEVELHIKE. The goal is to feed the TV the cleanest signal the console can produce.
3. **Match the video standard.** Confirm the converter supports the console's region (NTSC or PAL) so the signal syncs.
4. **Run a known-good HDMI cable** from the converter or cable's HDMI output to the TV. A basic certified high-speed cable like the Amazon Basics one above is plenty for retro resolutions, and it removes a flaky cable as a suspect if something looks wrong.
5. **Select the right HDMI input on the TV and enable Game Mode** in the picture settings to cut processing lag.
6. **Optionally add a clean-up pass.** If the picture works but looks soft or you want a sharper image, an inline upscaler such as the mClassic Retro Edition sits on the HDMI cable and applies a real-time processing pass, with the understood limit that it cannot recover detail the console never sent.

Work the path in order and the failure points are easy to isolate: no picture at all usually means a wrong input, an unsupported video standard, or a bad cable. A soft picture usually means composite where RGB was possible. A laggy picture usually means Game Mode is off or a frame-buffering converter is in the chain.

## How to connect an N64 to a modern TV without HDMI

The Nintendo 64 has **no HDMI output**, so the question is really how to get its analog signal onto a TV that only has HDMI inputs. The N64 outputs composite by default and S-Video on most models, both a step softer than RGB. The simplest path is a **composite-to-HDMI converter like the GANA box** above, which takes the yellow, white, and red RCA plugs and outputs HDMI scaled to 720p or 1080p. That gets a picture quickly, with the honest caveat that composite is the softest signal and the box adds its own processing.

For a cleaner result, use the console's **S-Video output** into a converter that accepts it, since S-Video separates brightness from color for a sharper image than composite. The catch with the N64 specifically is that smart TVs are often the worst at displaying its low-resolution signal directly, which is why a converter that outputs a standard 720p or 1080p HDMI signal usually behaves better than feeding the TV the raw analog feed. **Enable Game Mode** on the TV to cut processing lag once the picture is up.

## How to connect a SNES to a modern or smart TV

The Super Nintendo is one of the few retro consoles with a **clean, dedicated path**, because it can output RGB and there are console-specific cables that tap it. The targeted option is a **SNES-specific HDMI cable like the LEVELHIKE** above, which plugs into the console's multi-out, pulls the RGB signal rather than composite, and outputs HDMI with a 4:3 or 16:9 switch, no console modification required. That feeds a smart TV a cleaner starting signal than a generic composite converter.

If you would rather use a universal converter, the SNES also outputs composite into a box like the GANA, but you lose the RGB sharpness. The rule holds: **feed the cleanest signal the console can produce**, and on the SNES that means RGB over a dedicated cable when picture quality matters.

## How to connect a Genesis to a 4K TV

The Sega Genesis can output composite and, on many models, RGB over SCART, so the approach mirrors the others: **feed the cleanest signal it can produce**. For a quick connection to a 4K TV, a **composite-to-HDMI converter like the GANA box** gets a picture on screen and supports both PAL and NTSC sources, which matters for imported Genesis or Mega Drive units.

For a sharper result, an **RGB-capable path into a line multiplier like the OSSC** covered in the scalers guide produces cleaner, lower-lag output, though it needs a SCART or component RGB source rather than composite. On a 4K panel especially, **enabling Game Mode is the single most effective lag fix**, and a known-good high-speed HDMI cable rules out the cable as a suspect if the picture looks wrong.

## FAQ

**Why won't my N64 work on a smart TV?** Most current smart TVs have **no analog input at all**, so the N64's composite or S-Video signal has nothing to plug into directly, and even with an adapter some smart TVs struggle to lock onto its low-resolution 240p-class signal. The fix is a converter like the GANA box that turns the analog output into a standard 720p or 1080p HDMI signal the TV reliably accepts. Use S-Video into the converter if your N64 supports it for a cleaner picture than composite.

**Composite vs RGB vs HDMI, which for my console?** **Feed the cleanest signal your console can produce.** Composite (the yellow RCA plug) is the softest and is the floor most cartridge consoles can hit without modification. RGB or component is the sharpest analog output and is worth using whenever the console supports it, as the SNES does over a dedicated cable. HDMI is what the TV wants, reached either by converting the analog signal or by a console-specific cable that taps RGB at the source. The practical answer: **RGB if your console can do it, composite if that is all it has**.

**Will a $15 converter look bad?** It will look like composite, the softest analog signal, so on a large 4K panel it can look soft with some color bleed, per RetroRGB's framing of cheap composite-to-HDMI boxes as the **lowest-fidelity path**. That said, a budget converter like the GANA box is the **cheapest way to get a console working at all**, and for a composite-only console it is often the only option. If you want sharper, the upgrade is an RGB source feeding a better scaler, covered in the scalers guide, not a different cheap converter.
